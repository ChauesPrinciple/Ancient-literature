Medea: Background and Summary

Background

A Painting of Medea with Her Children

Euripides lived during the Golden Age of Athens from 484 B.C.E. to 406 B.C.E. Euripides was a philosopher and playwright whose works often challenged the social norms of his time. Although not a Sophist, Euripides incorporated elements of sophistic thinking into his plays, particularly the relationships among language, truth, and perception. His works are known for presenting controversial, radical ideas about human nature and society.

Medea won third prize at the Dionysia festival in 431 B.C.E., the same year the Peloponnesian War began. The Peloponnesian War was a conflict between Athens and the Peloponnesian League, led by Sparta. As tensions rose between the Greek city-states, the war created social and political upheavals, challenging the institutions of democracy and power that shaped Greek society. Medea, like other plays from this period, such as Lysistrata, reflects these societal tensions.

Jason, leader of the Argonauts, was given the task of capturing the Golden Fleece, a powerful and magical item. Jason sailed aboard the Argo, journeying to Colchis, where he met Medea, the daughter of King Aeëtes. Medea, a powerful sorceress, fell in love with Jason and helped him complete his quest, betraying her own family in the process. She and Jason fled to Corinth, where they lived in exile and had two sons. In Medea, Jason has abandoned Medea to marry Glauce, the daughter of King Creon of Corinth. Medea is enraged at being dishonored and abandoned.

Summary

In Medea, Euripides presents one of the most shocking female characters in literature. While Lady Macbeth famously contemplates killing her child, Medea takes that horrific step, murdering her two sons. The play is set in the ancient Greek city-state of Corinth. Jason, having left Medea, has married the Princess of Corinth. In the play's opening, Medea is grief-stricken and furious, lamenting her betrayal and abandonment.

Her desire for revenge drives the play’s action, and her hatred towards Jason is understandable. She sacrificed everything—her family, homeland, and future—for Jason, who now discards her for another woman. Medea’s decision to kill her children is driven by a desire to obliterate Jason’s happiness completely. If the children survive, Jason would still have a source of comfort. While Jason has undeniably wronged Medea, nothing justifies her ultimate decision to commit filicide. Medea’s internal struggle before the murder is chilling. She briefly wavers, torn by her maternal instincts, but her overwhelming hatred for Jason overpowers any remaining love for her sons.

Euripides argues that tragedy unfolds when hate becomes stronger than love. The play explores the consequences of unchecked rage, offering the following meditations:

  • "All men love themselves more than their neighbors."
  • "There is no justice in the eyes of mortal men. Everyone avoids a friend once he is a pauper."
  • "I’d sooner have great fame than houses filled with gold or the power to sing melodies sweeter than Orpheus."
  • "I pray that moderation, the gods' most beautiful gift, will always guide me."
  • "As for human life, it is nothing but shadows among human beings. No one is happy. Wealth may flow to make one man luckier than another, but no man is ever truly happy."

The chorus, comprised of Corinthian women who are Medea's friends, serves as the voice of Greek society. They try to console her, but Medea remains inconsolable as the play’s tension rises. King Creon arrives to banish Medea and her children, fearing she may harm his daughter. Medea pleads for one more day, and Creon reluctantly grants her request. In that brief time, Medea devises a deadly plot to murder Jason’s new wife.

Jason appears, offering to help Medea in her exile. Medea reminds him of all she sacrificed to aid him in his past endeavors. Jason dismisses her sacrifices, claiming he did more for her than she ever did for him. Medea scorns his offer, asserting that gifts from a traitor are worthless.

Feeling abandoned with no family, no home, and no hope, Medea resolves to carry out her deadly plan. Soon after, a messenger arrives from Creon’s palace to report that both the princess and Creon are dead. Medea’s poisoned robe and crown have consumed them in a fiery death. Medea then enters her house to kill her children. Their cries echo as she commits the unimaginable act.

Jason arrives too late, hearing that Creon and the princess are dead. He rushes to stop Medea but finds his sons already slain. In the play’s resolution, Medea escapes on a winged chariot, a gift from her grandfather Helios, carrying the bodies of her children. As she flies away, she taunts Jason, leaving him devastated and powerless.
