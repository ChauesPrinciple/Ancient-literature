Medea: Professor's Analysis

Includes assessment.

In our analysis of Medea, we’ve seen how Euripides dramatizes what happens when someone steps outside of society’s expected roles. Medea’s actions disrupt the norms of family, loyalty, and duty — and she is ultimately portrayed as dangerous, even monstrous. Similarly, in Beowulf, we discussed how diplomacy, gift-giving, and peace-making — behaviors that might not fit the stereotypical image of a warrior — play a crucial role in maintaining stability between the Geats and the Danes.

To set the stage, here’s a short introduction to how social norms work to shape behavior and enforce conformity:

How do stories like Medea and Beowulf use role-breaking behaviors — whether defiance, decisiveness, or peace-making — to comment on who is allowed to hold power? Why might storytellers portray characters who step outside expected norms as disruptive or even monstrous? And considering that some ancient cultures celebrated alternative models of leadership (such as matrilineal traditions or alliance-builders), what does this suggest about the role of myth and epic in reinforcing — or challenging — social order?
