Gilgamesh: Professor's Analysis

The outer walls of Uruk, built by Gilgamesh.

At its core, Gilgamesh is the first and prototypical "Hero's Journey," so it should come as no surprise that this text deals explicitly with the idea of “heroic systems.” The Ninevite version of the epic begins with a prologue in praise of Gilgamesh, who is two-thirds divine and one-third human, the great builder, warrior, and knower of all things on land and sea.

"Man is neither angel nor beast and unhappily whoever wants to act the angel acts the beast." — Blaise Pascal

Gilgamesh's conflict is a central conflict of our species, and when we meet him, he is very much the beast.

In books like The Hero with a Thousand Faces and The Power of Myth, Joseph Campbell reports on the deep structure of narrative that he found while comparing the myths and legends of many cultures using inductive reasoning. “The Hero’s Journey” was his all-embracing metaphor for the deep inner journey of transformation that heroes in every time and place seem to share: a path that leads them through great movements of departure, initiation, and return.

The Hero's Journey

I. Departure

1
Status Quo
Gilgamesh rules Uruk as a tyrant, oppressing his people and abusing his power.
2
Call to Adventure
The creation of Enkidu and the subsequent decision to travel to the Cedar Forest for fame.
3
Assistance
Gilgamesh receives counsel from the Elders and prayers from his mother, Ninsun.
4
Departure
Gilgamesh and Enkidu leave the safety of Uruk, crossing the threshold into the wild.

II. Initiation

5
Trials
They defeat Humbaba and the Bull of Heaven, proving their physical might.
6
Approach
Enkidu dies. Gilgamesh is struck by the terror of death and seeks Utnapishtim.
7
CRISIS
Gilgamesh crosses the Waters of Death and fails the test of sleep (mortality).
8
Treasure
He obtains the plant of "How-the-Old-Man-Becomes-Young" (literal immortality).
9
Result
A snake steals the plant while he bathes. He has lost his chance at eternal youth.

III. Return

10
Return
Gilgamesh returns to Uruk, empty-handed and weary, accompanied by Urshanabi.
11
New Life
He sees the walls of Uruk not just as brick, but as his enduring legacy (Symbolic Immortality).
12
Resolution
He inscribes his story on stone. He becomes the wise king who knows the ways of the world.

The Beast & The Hero

Gilgamesh's journey is not merely a physical trek across the world; it is an internal evolution from raw, unchecked impulse to tempered, civilized wisdom. When we first meet Gilgamesh, "he is a bit of a bastard." He is described as two-thirds divine and one-third human, a volatile ratio that manifests in tyranny. He abuses his power, claiming droit du seigneur (the right of the lord) over new brides and pressing men into labor. He is, effectively, a beast in king's clothing—ruled by appetite and ego, rather than responsibility.

The intervention of the gods introduces Enkidu, a man of the wild who is Gilgamesh's mirror image. Where Gilgamesh is a civilized man acting like a beast, Enkidu is a beast who must become a man. Their wrestling match in the streets of Uruk is the pivotal moment of "taming" for both. Enkidu checks Gilgamesh's tyranny, and Gilgamesh gives Enkidu purpose. Yet, even as friends, their early exploits—killing Humbaba and the Bull of Heaven—are driven by a juvenile desire for fame. They are trying to write their names in history through violence, a crude form of immortality.

Becker's "Heroic Systems"

To understand what happens next, we must look to the 20th-century cultural anthropologist Ernest Becker. In his Pulitzer Prize-winning book The Denial of Death, Becker argues that the driving force behind human behavior is the terror of death. Because we are intelligent enough to realize we will die, we spend our lives constructing "immortality projects" or "heroic systems"—cultural structures that allow us to feel that we are part of something eternal, something that will outlast our physical bodies.

"Man is a worm and food for worms. This is the paradox: he is out of nature and hopelessly in it; he is dual, up in the stars and yet housed in a heart-pumping, breath-gasping body that belongs to a fish and still has gill-slits... to have to die is a terrifying thought." — Ernest Becker

Gilgamesh begins the epic terrified of this paradox, though he masks it with bravado. His early quest for fame is his initial, immature "heroic system." He attempts to create a literal immortality through deeds so great that "his name will be stamped on brick." But this system collapses the moment Enkidu dies. When Gilgamesh sees the worm crawl out of his friend's nose, the "terror" Becker describes shatters his defenses. Fame is no longer enough; he needs to physically conquer death.

The Failure of Literal Immortality

Gilgamesh's journey across the Waters of Death to find Utnapishtim is a desperate attempt to find a loophole in the human condition. He explicitly seeks literal immortality—to live forever in the flesh. But the epic systematically strips him of this hope. He fails the test of staying awake (a metaphor for death), and even when he obtains the rejuvenating plant, it is stolen by a snake (a symbol of nature reclaiming its own).

This failure is crucial. If Gilgamesh had succeeded, the story would have no meaning for us. In purely Beckerian terms, his "Return" is the acceptance that literal immortality is impossible for humans. We are, inescapable, food for worms.

Achieving Symbolic Immortality

The profound resolution of the epic occurs not when Gilgamesh returns to Uruk defeated, but when he looks at the walls he built. In that moment, he shifts from a quest for literal immortality to an acceptance of symbolic immortality. He realizes that while he will die, his creations—the walls of Uruk, the laws he established, and most importantly, the story inscribed on the stone tablet—will endure.

He transitions from a "beast" fighting against the natural order to a true "hero" who upholds the cultural illusion that gives life meaning. He accepts the "useful delusion" of civilization: that what we build matters. By writing his story down, he grants himself the only immortality humans can actually have: remembrance. In doing so, he completes the Hero's Journey not as a god who defied death, but as a man who learned how to live within it.
