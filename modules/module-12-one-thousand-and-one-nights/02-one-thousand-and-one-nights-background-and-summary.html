One Thousand and One Nights: Background and Summary

Background

Sheikh Ibrahim and His Visitors

To give background for One Thousand and One Nights, we must begin, not in ancient Persia where the stories originated, but in 17th and 18th-century France. The central figure in this story is Antoine Galland (1646–1715), a French scholar employed by the King of France to acquire antiquities and manuscripts from the Near East. Galland was also a linguist, traveler, diplomat, and what was known as an Orientalist. Galland introduced Western audiences to The Nights, making him one of the key figures responsible for the spread of these tales in Europe.

Medieval Arab authors knew about One Thousand and One Nights, but they did not celebrate it as a literary masterpiece. A 10th-century historian Al-Masudi described it as a collection of “silly stories about the Kings of Old” and mentioned that the tales had originally been written in Persian, Sanskrit, and Greek. He explicitly named a Persian collection called Hazār Afsāna (“A Thousand Tales”), which was later translated into Arabic. At this point, the collection was simply called One Thousand Nights and did not yet have the additional night. The familiar framing story of Scheherazade and her sister, Dunyazad, was already present in this earlier version.

Galland, while working in the Middle East in the late 17th century, collected various manuscripts and learned Arabic, Persian, and Turkish. In 1698, he translated the stories of Sinbad the Sailor from their cycle, but mistakenly believed they were part of The Nights. No manuscript of One Thousand and One Nights included the Sinbad tales until Galland’s French translation, which later led to their inclusion in subsequent Arabic editions. Thus, when Sinbad appears in modern versions of The Nights, it is due to Galland’s decision to incorporate these stories.

Galland eventually obtained a Syrian manuscript of The Nights dating back to the 15th century. This manuscript contained only 35 tales across 271 nights, far fewer than the 1,001 nights implied by the title. To meet the expectations of his audience, Galland added several stories from other sources, including the Sinbad tales, as well as new stories recounted to him by a Syrian Christian informant named Hanna Diab. Among these stories were Aladdin and Ali Baba and the Forty Thieves—two tales that do not appear in any earlier Arabic manuscript but were first included in Galland’s French version. In fact, these stories likely originated with Galland, further demonstrating how his translation was less of a direct transfer from Arabic texts and more of an adaptation.

Galland’s translation, Les Mille et Une Nuits, was published between 1704 and 1717 and became a sensation in Europe. His work was quickly translated into English, and most Western versions of The Nights are derived from Galland’s edition. While later translations would claim to be based on Arabic sources, most were simply reworkings of Galland’s text with some added materials. His translation introduced Europe to The Nights, but it was far from a faithful reproduction of the original Syrian manuscript.

Summary

One Thousand and One Nights consists of numerous tales, with the exact number varying depending on how they are counted, but the collection begins with a framing story.

The frame story tells of King Shahryar, who, after discovering his wife’s infidelity, becomes convinced that all women are treacherous. He decides to marry a different maiden every night and execute her the next morning. Shahryar’s kingdom is soon in revolt as the supply of available young women dwindles. His vizier, tasked with procuring these women, has two daughters: Scheherazade and Dunyazad.

Scheherazade, determined to end the king’s reign of terror, volunteers to be his next bride. On their wedding night, she requests that her younger sister, Dunyazad, be allowed to visit so that Scheherazade can say goodbye. After the marriage, Dunyazad arrives, and Scheherazade begins to tell a story. However, she leaves it unfinished by dawn, compelling the king to spare her life for another day so he can hear the conclusion.

This cycle continues for 1,001 nights. Every night, Scheherazade tells a tale, often embedding stories within stories, a narrative structure known as mise en abyme (stories within stories). For example, one night she tells the tale of The Hunchback, which leads to the tale of the Tailor, and then to the tale of the Barber. The stories vary from comedies to tragedies, romances to fables, all interwoven with themes of love, justice, faith, and betrayal.

Over time, Scheherazade’s storytelling changes Shahryar’s heart. By the end of the 1,001 nights, he falls in love with her and abandons his vengeful decree, making Scheherazade his queen. Through her tales, Scheherazade not only saves her own life but also transforms Shahryar into a wise and just ruler.
