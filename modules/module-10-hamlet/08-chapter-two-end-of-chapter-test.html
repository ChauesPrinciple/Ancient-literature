Purpose:

The purpose of this section of the test is to demonstrate, first, that you have read and understood one text that we have read this semester; you get to choose which two summaries you wish to complete.

Criteria: 

Summarizing Shorter Texts (ten pages or fewer)

  • Write a one-sentence summary of each paragraph.
  • Formulate a single sentence that summarizes the whole text.
  • Write a paragraph (or more): begin with the overall summary sentence and follow it with the paragraph summary sentences.
  • Rearrange and rewrite the paragraph to make it clear and concise, to eliminate repetition and relatively minor points, and to provide transitions. The final version should be a complete, unified, and coherent.

Summarizing Longer Texts (more than ten pages)

  • Outline the text. Break it down into its major sections—groups of paragraphs focused on a common topic—and list the main supporting points for each section.
  • Write a one or two sentence summary of each section.
  • Formulate a single sentence to summarize the whole text, looking at the author's thesis or topic sentences as a guide.
  • Write a paragraph (or more): begin with the overall summary sentence and follow it with the section summary sentences.
  • Rewrite and rearrange your paragraph(s) as needed to make your writing clear and concise, to eliminate relatively minor or repetitious points, and to provide transitions. Make sure your summary includes all the major supporting points of each idea. The final version should be a complete, unified, and coherent.

Task:

Please summarize The Iliad from this chapter and provide a summary of one of of the major points of our reading; please note that a summary encompasses the main details or ideas only, and does not convey the entire narrative.

Purpose:

Close reading is deep analysis of how a literary text works; it is both a reading process and something you include in a literary analysis paper, though in a refined form. Fiction writers and poets build texts out of many central components, including subject, form, and specific word choices. Literary analysis involves examining these components, which allows us to find in small parts of the text clues to help us understand the whole.

Task:

For the task of close reading, one assumption is critical: a rich text simply cannot be understood and appreciated by a single read, no matter how skilled and motivated the reader.

To complete the first task please use the steps listed below to analyze a specific passage of your choosing, during your close analysis: please keep in mind, we cannot analyze these varied aspects of the text simultaneously:

  1. Determine central ideas or themes of a text and analyze their development; summarize the key supporting details and ideas.
  2. Analyze how and why individuals, events, and ideas develop and interact over the course of a text.
  3. Interpret words and phrases as they are used in a text, including determining technical, connotative, and figurative meanings, and analyze how specific word choices shape meaning or tone.
  4. Analyze the structure of texts, including how specific sentences, paragraphs, and larger portions of the text (e.g., a section, chapter, scene, or stanza) relate to each other and the whole.
  5. Assess how point of view or purpose shapes the content and style of a text.

Criteria: 

A completed close reading completes each step of both the first and second task, in two paragraphs:
Paragraph One (general) should answer the following:

  1. Determine central ideas or themes of a text and analyze their development; summarize the key supporting details and ideas.
  2. Analyze how and why individuals, events, and ideas develop and interact over the course of a text.

Paragraph Two (creation/extension) should answer the following:

  1. Interpret words and phrases as they are used in a text, including determining technical, connotative, and figurative meanings, and analyze how specific word choices shape meaning or tone.
  2. Analyze the structure of texts, including how specific sentences, paragraphs, and larger portions of the text (e.g., a section, chapter, scene, or stanza) relate to each other and the whole.
  3. Assess how point of view or purpose shapes the content and style of a text.

Criteria

Please follow these minimum requirements to earn credit:

  1. Two complete, well formed paragraphs, including:
    1. An introduction that ends with an evocative thesis that directly connects the text and the contemporary issue,
    2. A body paragraph that provides evidence, including quotes and paraphrases with specific detail about the texts, and
  2. Textual evidence from the text, including specific details, descriptions, quotations, etc., to prove and give evidence that what you are saying is true.
    1. You need to clearly demonstrate that you have read, understood, and analyzed the literature.
  3. Correct MLA formatting, including in text citations, signal phrases, and punctuation.
  4. Correct application of literary terms, for example, identifying the protagonist, antagonist, where we are in the plot structure, e.g., climax, whether the character is sympathetic, or what kind of irony or satire we are talking about.

Task:

Introduction:

  1. Grab the Readers' attention (Do not begin by immediately talking about the text)
    1. A synopsis of the text does not belong in the introduction.
    2. The purpose of the introduction is to set up your argument, not talk about the text.
  2. Set up your topic – establish the contemporary issue and why it is an issue.
    1. Why should anyone care about this issue you are relating the text to?
    2. Focus on the issue you have identified and explore it deeply. Do not list numerous vague examples.
  3. Craft a thesis that is an argument:
    1. Beowulf suggests that we need to take a deeper look into the influence of Christianity on the subjugation of women and their agency because…
    2. (This Text) demonstrates through allegorical means our need to… (argument)… because…
    3. When we look at (this issue), it may seem new to us, but, in fact, if we look more deeply into (text of choice), we find that a reevaluation of this issue is necessary because…
    4. On the surface, (text of choice) seems to be about (theme), yet it holds an important lesson on (topic of argument) for our current generation in regard to…

Body of the Essay:

  1. Give a synopsis or summary of the relevant details from the text.
    1. This should be a broad overview.
  2. Reestablish how the text is related to the contemporary issue you feel needs/deserves more attention/a different understand/a different approach.
    1. This includes establishing how/why the issue is important, to whom it is important, and why we should care. This is where you should be specific.
  3. Analyze, in depth, a specific section of the text that relates directly to the contemporary issue at hand.
    1. This is an in-depth analysis of the text and its inferred meaning.
      1. Within this section there should be close reading, quotations,       paraphrases, and specific observations of the text that demonstrate      a clear understanding and advanced analysis of the text.
    2. Next, you should also establish the connection with the contemporary issue and the text(s) you have chosen to analyze.
      1. Think of this along lines of comparison – draw direct parallels       between to tension of the text and the tension of the issue (as a side note, please make sure to employ parallelism here).
  4. Application of the text to the contemporary issue: how does the new understanding/method/change provided by the text change our approach to the contemporary issue?
    1. This demonstrates the overall relevance of this text to our current culture.

Reflect & Discuss

Engage with the text and your peers using the reading questions above.
