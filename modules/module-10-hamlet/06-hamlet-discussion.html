Hamlet

As we are now more than halfway through the course material, you have probably noticed a lack of stories from some continents and cultures, such as Africa and the Americas. This is partially due to the lack of free translations. Still, it is also indicative of a Western perspective that because these cultures utilized an oral tradition to record their myths and history, they had no history.

What does this have to do with Hamlet?

In the analysis for Hamlet, I asked this rhetorical question:

"What would happen if our "conscience" (our internal morality) and "ego ideal" (what people we respect say is moral) are in conflict?" -- Here is a primer on these terms:

The purpose of asking this question was to highlight how the conflict between the conscience and the ego-ideal results in diverse forms of self-punishment, the "moral masochism" Freud described in terms "the criminal from a sense of guilt," "those wrecked by success," and other self-sabotaging and self-tormenting character-types. There is, however, a nice contrast to Hamlet that comes from the Congo told by the Nyanga people called the Epic of Mwindo. Please watch this video explaining the Mwindo epic:

Within the Mwindo epic, and similar to the notion of dharma in the Bhagavad Gita, there is an emphasis on not seeking vengeance and maintaining a sense of humility even in greatness. Thus, Mwindo does not seek to kill anyone out of vengeance, and he does not wish to slay his father for his transgressions.

Would you please compare and contrast Hamlet's and Mwindo's responses to the "Betrayal of What is Right" within their community? Why does Hamlet suffer from a subconscious need for punishment that leads to his tragic end, and what could he learn from Mwindo?
