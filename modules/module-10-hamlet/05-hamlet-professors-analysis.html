Hamlet: Professor's Analysis

Hamlet may not seem like an obvious choice for this chapter on warriors. He is notorious for his inaction, right? By some, maybe, but let us dig a bit deeper into the action of the play, and whether Hamlet is truly as fickle as he has been accused.

Thesis: It is the position of this analysis that Hamlet is acting rationally; he is also the first model of the modern existential dilemma that we, and our soldiers, face.

Honor Culture vs. Guilt Culture

In most of our texts, we are dealing with honor-based societies. This is true for Gilgamesh, the Iliad, Beowulf, and the Bhagavad Gita, but distinctly not the Song of Roland or Hamlet, which fall under the Guilt Culture area. In Beowulf and the Song of Roland, we see the bookending of the transitional period between the honor-based warrior culture and the modern guilt-based Christian society of our modern world. Hamlet, on the other hand, shows us the inherent internal conflict of the modern person:

Is the warrior ethos of revenge or the Christian ethos of forgiveness of higher-order concern?

Freudian Framework: Id, Ego, and Superego

Naturally, to explore this internal conflict, we will need to define some terms of the mind, via psychoanalysis:

Id — Instinctual Drives Present at Birth

  • Does not distinguish between reality and fantasy
  • Operates according to the pleasure principle—drive toward immediate gratification
  • Sources of energy:
    • Eros—life instinct perpetuates life
    • Libido—sexual energy or motivation
    • Thanatos—death instinct, aggression, self-destructive actions

Ego — Develops Out of the Id in Infancy

  • Understands reality and logic
  • A mediator between the id and superego
  • Rational, organized, logical, and mediator to demands of reality
  • Reality principle—the ability to postpone gratification in accordance with demands of reality
  • Can repress desires that cannot be met in an acceptable manner

Superego — Internalization of Societal & Parental Moral Standards

  • One's conscience; focuses on what the person "should" do
  • Develops around ages 5-6
  • Partially unconscious
  • Can be harshly punitive using feelings of guilt
  • The superego has two parts:
    • The Conscience: Information about things viewed as bad by parents and society
    • The Ego Ideal: Rules and standards for behaviors that the ego aspires to

Key Insight: The superego is the primary culprit in this analysis. In introductory courses, the id, ego, and superego are often presented simplistically: with the id as the "devil on one shoulder" and the superego as "the angel on the other." This is only true, however, if our intuition is always wrong and society is always right. Certainly, we can agree this is not the case.

The Conflict Within

Our discontent in civilization arises not because of a heightening of our sense of guilt but rather through the heightening of the unconscious need for punishment that defends against the sense of guilt. It is beneficial to reflect on the Song of Roland and Dr. Shay's assessment of REMFs, to understand how social structures can stand in the way of a soldier's ability to feel guilt.

What would happen if our "conscience" (our internal morality) and "ego ideal" (what people we respect say is moral) were in conflict?

Since most of us do not possess the strength of character for conscious suppression and self-mastery without lying to ourselves, most people, including our soldiers, will be forced to fall back on repression, with the disguised return of the repressed that repression itself inevitably entails.

The disguised return of the repressed guilt is often manifested in the form of the sadistic superego redirecting the id's aggression away from the object world (e.g., the REMFs who contradicted the moral code) and against the ego (ourselves). This results in various forms of self-punishment; Freud described the "moral masochism" in terms of "the criminal from a sense of guilt," "those wrecked by success," and other self-sabotaging and self-tormenting character-types.

Hamlet's "Failure to Act"

Do we see this illustrated in Shakespeare's work when we apply Dr. Shay's viewpoint? The feeling of guilt that might accompany the state of being guilty is absent because this feeling is unbearable or unacceptable. In many ways, we have not seen a character who struggles with such a modern dilemma:

  • It was not morally "wrong" for Achilles to slay Hector; it was tragic.
  • Nor was it "illegal" for Beowulf to face the dragon, but the lack of belief and support led to his demise.
  • For Hamlet, the death of his father demands vengeance, but it is also immoral for him to kill as a modern Christian man.

Let us tackle the subject of Hamlet's "failure to act." We need to look at three key scenes:

  1. Hamlet slaying Rosencrantz and Guildenstern: When he finds the letter demanding his own death upon arrival in England, he does not hesitate to have his former friends take his place in death.
  2. Hamlet slaying Polonius: When Hamlet confronts his mother in her chambers and believes it is his uncle behind the tapestry, Hamlet does not hesitate for a moment—he stabs Polonius without even checking who is truly hiding there.
  3. The "ineffectual" scene: Where Hamlet fails to kill Claudius while Claudius is praying.

These first two scenes do not paint a picture of a man who is afraid of violence, cruelty, or action. So, why does he fail to kill Claudius when he has the chance? Hamlet himself tells us:

He took my father grossly, full of bread, With all his crimes broad blown, as flush as May; And how his audit stands who knows save heaven. But in our circumstance and course of thought 'Tis heavy with him. And am I then revenged To take him in the purging of his soul, When he is fit and seasoned for his passage? No. (III.iii)

Hamlet is concerned with justice, especially with respect to his guilt-driven culture. The punishment must be fair, or at least feel fair. This does not reflect an inability to act; it reflects a complex rumination on the justness of Claudius' death.

The "To Be or Not To Be" Soliloquy

This modern moral dilemma is played out in the most famous soliloquy:

To be or not to be—that is the question: Whether 'tis nobler in the mind to suffer The slings and arrows of outrageous fortune, Or to take arms against a sea of troubles And, by opposing, end them. To die, to sleep— … To sleep, perchance to dream. Ay, there's the rub, For in that sleep of death what dreams may come, When we have shuffled off this mortal coil, Must give us pause... … Thus conscience does make cowards of us all, And thus the native hue of resolution Is sicklied o'er with the pale cast of thought, And enterprises of great pitch and moment With this regard their currents turn awry And lose the name of action.

The central question here is one we are all familiar with. While it feels right and fair to do something, it is wrong or against the law; therefore, we do not act. Does our inaction make us all ineffectual? No. However, it does highlight the need for repression and how such a conflict results in someone, like a soldier who has PTSD, punishing themselves even when they are not guilty.

Unlike Hamlet, the soldiers in previous examples did act. As noted in the "Betrayal of What's Right," the schism between doing something they knew was morally wrong and being rewarded for that action led to the downward spiral that PTSD represents. Such repressed guilt cannot be recognized, so it is unleashed in other ways. For Hamlet, it is the sabotaging of his relationship with Ophelia. For soldiers, it is often manifested in alcoholism, nightmares, and suicide.

Speaking Up for the Women: Ophelia

I cannot transition into the next module on women without speaking up for the women in Shakespeare's most famous tragedy. First, Ophelia, the tragic heroine, is often seen as having no agency. While it is true that she has very little and that she is treated rather poorly, there is more to her scenes than may meet the eye.

Specifically, in Act Four, Scene Five, when Ophelia states:

There's rosemary, that's for remembrance. Pray you, love, remember. And there is pansies, that's for thoughts. … There's fennel for you, and columbines. There's rue for you, and here's some for me; we may call it herb of grace o' Sundays. You must wear your rue with a difference. There's a daisy. I would give you some violets, but they withered all when my father died. They say he made a good end.

This, perhaps, sounds like madness to us, but consider poor Ophelia and how much she can or cannot say in her position without punishment. In English history, especially around the Elizabethan era, flowers had a great deal of symbolism:

🌿 Rosemary and Pansies
Given to Laertes; cited as being for remembrance and thoughts.
🌿 Fennel and Columbine
Given to King Claudius—a bold message: Fennel symbolizes flattery, and columbine is the flower for "deceived lovers," a symbol of male adultery and faithlessness.
🌿 Rue
Symbol for bitterness, thought to cause abortions in that day, and often connected with adultery. Given to Queen Gertrude, and Ophelia keeps some for herself.
🌼 Daisy
Ophelia picks up and sets down the daisy without giving it to anyone. The daisy symbolizes innocence and gentleness—evidently, Ophelia thought there was no place for innocence in the Danish court anymore.
💜 Violets
Ophelia says she would have brought violets but they all withered when her father died—the symbol of faithfulness and fidelity.

In her own way, Ophelia was calling out everyone in the room for what they were. Curiously, however, Claudius does not seem to get the worst of it. Gertrude is being accused of adultery, bitterness, and abortion. It is as if Ophelia is telling Gertrude that she knows Gertrude tried to kill her own son.

A Reinterpretation: The True Antagonist?

Furthermore, consider Ophelia's lines just before the latter passage:

You must sing "A-down a-down"—and you "Call him a-down-a."—O, how the wheel becomes it! It is the false steward that stole his master's daughter.

"It is the false steward that stole his master's daughter." It begs the question, who is the falsest person in the play?

It is the traditional viewpoint that Claudius is the mastermind and antagonist of this play, but I challenge that this may be due to the focus on Hamlet as someone who suffers from the Oedipus complex—a Freudian concept of a child's rivalry towards his father due to unconscious psychosexual affection towards his mother. If, however, Hamlet's failure to act is instead due to the conflict within his superego, then this interpretation is not necessarily true.

Furthermore, if we examine Claudius' prayer scene a little more closely, it is strange that he feels such remorse if it were his plan to kill his brother. Shakespeare does not shy away from a strong woman antagonist (see Lady Macbeth), and it is worth considering that right after Ophelia makes this pronouncement, she "dies" by "possible suicide" when climbing on a branch, and the Queen watches as she slowly sinks and her dress slowly splays out in the stream. Gertrude does not mention attempting to save Ophelia even once.

Perhaps she wanted a man she could control.
