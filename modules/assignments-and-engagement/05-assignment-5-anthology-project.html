Assignment #5: Anthology Project

Assignment #4: Anthology Project

Instructions

Purpose:

The purpose of this project is to not only analyze texts, but to frame a comparative context (the context that brings the texts together, similar to our modules) to assess the ideas, forces, and values that shape the texts, and to practice research using scholarly and peer-reviewed sources, practice MLA documentation and formatting, and use proper grammar and spelling.

Criteria:

Minimum requirements to earn credit:

  • Proposal: 1-2 pages as described below
  • Sources: At least 10 sources as described below
  • Length: At least 5 pages + Works Cited page as described below
  • Documentation: Correct MLA documentation is expected, including in-text citations and Works Cited. If you include any source material without giving proper credit, you risk either earning a zero on the assignment or an F as your final grade in the course.

Task:

You will select one of the themes we have outlined throughout the three distinct modules to explore in your anthology. You will not actually assemble a new anthology textbook; instead, your anthology project will contain the following elements:

  • Cover or Title Page: (1 page)
    • an original title
    • editor's (your) name
  • Table of Contents: (1-2 pages)
    • List the introduction, authors/titles of all works in the anthology, and any other elements you included.
    • Make up some page numbers.
    • You may wish to use headings to clarify the text’s organization.
  • Preface: (at least 3 pages + additional Works Cited page)
    • Attract the reader’s attention and explain why readers should care about this theme or the specific stories you have chosen to include. What does it show about its historical context, what does it illustrate about warriors, PTSD, psychological motivation, how men view women or humanity, why is it interesting or relevant? 
    • Explain what is to come, articulating the unifying themes or ordering principles of the anthology.
    • Additional body paragraphs should discuss how each text contributes to the reader’s understanding of this monster or theme. Compare/contrast texts, and justify your inclusion of each one. Incorporate brief quotations, paraphrase, and/or summary from the texts (with in-text citations) to support your points. You may analyze one text per paragraph or multiple texts per paragraph, but you must analyze all texts, and the organization must be clear.
    • Conclude, and explain what readers will learn from this anthology. Persuade the reader to purchase this anthology by mentioning interesting features or content. Be specific!
    • Works Cited page
    • Use an academic voice, and use a third-person point of view (no “you” or “I”). You may refer to the editor if necessary.
    • This is not a traditional essay with a thesis; I suggest looking at examples of introductory essays or prefaces in other anthologies.
  • Extras: (optional – you may include any of these elements for extra credit)
    • Dedication
    • Foreword
    • Epilogue
    • About the editor
    • Images, charts, or maps (with correct MLA documentation)
    • Headnotes
    • Discussion questions
    • List of recommendations for further reading
    • Glossary
    • Index

Proposal: (1-2 pages)

  • State what monster or theme you chose and explain why you chose it. You may use first-person (“I”) point of view.
  • Include a list of texts you are considering. Some of these texts may eventually change, but the proposal should demonstrate that you have determined your topic and have made progress on the work of evaluating and selecting texts.
  • You may also include any questions or problems you are having – what feedback from others would help you?
  • Submit your proposal to the NS Online Discussion Board called “Proposal for Anthology Project” before class. Afterward, your instructor and classmates will review your proposal and offer comments and suggestions.
  • The proposal is required; you cannot earn any points on the Anthology Project unless you first submit a proposal. Late proposals may be submitted to the Discussion Board, but they will lose late points according to the syllabus policy, and you will miss the opportunity for feedback.

Sources:  

  • You should include at least 10 texts.
  • Texts must be previously published literary works: short stories, essays, articles, plays, poetry, songs, or excerpts from longer works (like novels). Since you will not have to create a physical book, you may also include films, specific episodes of television shows, video games, or other multimedia.
  • You should avoid all biographies, plot summaries, dictionaries, encyclopedias, and most websites. If you have questions about whether a source is appropriate, ask.
  • You may use all primary sources, all secondary sources, or a combination.
  • You must feature multiple authors; you may select a maximum of three texts by the same author.
