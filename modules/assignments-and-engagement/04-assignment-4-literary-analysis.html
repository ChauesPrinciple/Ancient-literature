Assignment #4: Literary Analysis

Assignment #4: Literary Analysis

Instructions

Purpose:

The purpose of the literary analysis is to demonstrate the relevance of one of the ancient texts from the course, that is also part of your Anthology Project, in relation to a modern issue, problem, or situation that you have identified. This is part of your research.

If a text is no longer relevant, then it no longer serves a real purpose.

Your goal is to connect with one text in a meaningful way and to demonstrate the relevance of that text to a modern dilemma. Furthermore, the essay should demonstrate how the text comments on, provides a solution to, or adds important nuance to the identified issue.

  • Please note that each module has a different focus, so your analysis should be engaging the text with respects to its module.

Criteria:

Minimum requirements to earn credit:

  • Proposal: 1 page as described below
  • Sources: At least 2 sources as described below
  • Length: At least 3 pages + Works Cited page as described below
  • Documentation: Correct MLA documentation is expected, including in-text citations and Works Cited. If you include any source material without giving proper credit, you risk either earning a zero on the assignment or an F as your final grade in the course.

Task:

Proposals:

Proposal: (This is a discussion!)

  • State what monster or theme you chose and explain why you chose it. You may use first-person (“I”) point of view.
  • Include a list of texts you are considering. Some of these texts may eventually change, but the proposal should demonstrate that you have determined your topic and have made progress on the work of evaluating and selecting texts.
  • You may also include any questions or problems you are having – what feedback from others would help you?
  • Submit your proposal to the NS Online Discussion Board called “Proposal for Anthology Project”. Afterward, your instructor and classmates will review your proposal and offer comments and suggestions.
  • The proposal is required; you cannot earn any points on the Anthology Project unless you first submit a proposal. Late proposals may be submitted to the Discussion Board, but they will lose late points according to the syllabus policy, and you will miss the opportunity for feedback.

Introduction:

  1. Grab the Readers’ attention (Do not begin by immediately talking about the text)
    • A synopsis of the text does not belong in the introduction.
    • The purpose of the introduction is to set up your argument, not talk about the text.
  2. Set up your topic – establish the contemporary issue and why it is an issue.
    • Why should anyone care about this issue you are relating the text to?
  3. Establish how/why the text relates to this issue only.
  4. Craft a thesis that is an argument:
    • Beowulf suggests that we need to take a deeper look into the influence of Christianity on the subjugation of women and their agency because…
    • (This Text) demonstrates through allegorical means our need to… (argument)… because…
    • When we look at (this issue), it may seem new to us, but, in fact, if we look more deeply into (text of choice), we find that a reevaluation of this issue is necessary because…
    • On the surface, (text of choice) seems to be about (theme), yet it holds an important lesson on (topic of argument) for our current generation in regards to…

Body:

Please note that the numbers in the bullet do not indicate the number of paragraphs necessary. You should not only have one paragraph of analysis!

  1. Provide background information and any summary that a reader who has not read the text will need to understand your analysis.
  2. Introduce your scholarly source, why you are including it, and what it helps us understand about the text.
  3. Deepen your analysis by connecting the text and your textual analysis to the contemporary issue you have indicated and connecting the text within your thesis.
  4. Apply your analysis to the contemporary source you have identified with the issue you are exploring.
